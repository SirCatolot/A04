Renaissance

The Renaissance, meaning "rebirth" in French, was a period of renewed interest in art, science, and learning that began in Italy during the 14th century and spread throughout Europe. This cultural movement marked the transition from the Medieval Period to the Early Modern Age.

Time Period: The Renaissance spanned roughly from the 14th to the 17th century (1300-1600), with the High Renaissance occurring during the late 15th to early 16th centuries.
Art and Architecture

Renaissance art revolutionized visual expression through realistic perspective, human anatomy study, and classical themes. Artists like Leonardo da Vinci, Michelangelo, and Raphael created masterpieces that still inspire us today.

Leonardo da Vinci (1452-1519): The ultimate Renaissance man, excelling as a painter, inventor, scientist, and engineer. His works include the Mona Lisa and The Last Supper.
Scientific Revolution

The Renaissance fostered scientific inquiry and observation. Scholars like Galileo Galilei and Nicolaus Copernicus challenged medieval beliefs about the universe, while anatomists like Andreas Vesalius advanced medical knowledge.

Galileo Galilei (1564-1642): Improved the telescope and made astronomical observations that supported the heliocentric model of the solar system.
Humanism and Literature

Renaissance humanism emphasized human potential and achievement. Writers like William Shakespeare, Dante Alighieri, and Niccolò Machiavelli produced works that explored human nature and society.

William Shakespeare (1564-1616): English playwright and poet whose works, including Hamlet and Romeo and Juliet, remain central to world literature.

The Renaissance's emphasis on individual achievement, classical learning, and empirical observation laid the groundwork for the modern world. Its innovations in art, science, and philosophy continue to influence our understanding of human potential and creativity.
