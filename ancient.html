Ancient Civilizations

Ancient civilizations laid the foundation for human society as we know it today. These early cultures developed writing systems, established governments, created monumental architecture, and made groundbreaking discoveries in mathematics, astronomy, and medicine that continue to influence our world.

Timeline: Ancient civilizations flourished from approximately 3500 BCE to 500 CE, spanning over 4,000 years of human development and innovation.
Ancient Egypt (c. 3100 - 30 BCE)

The civilization of the Nile River gave us the pyramids, hieroglyphics, and mummification. Egyptian pharaohs ruled for over 3,000 years, creating one of the longest-lasting civilizations in history. Their contributions to medicine, mathematics, and engineering were revolutionary.

Ancient Greece (c. 800 - 146 BCE)

The birthplace of democracy, philosophy, and the Olympic Games. Greek thinkers like Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle laid the foundations of Western philosophy, while mathematicians like Euclid and Archimedes made discoveries still taught today.

Roman Empire (c. 753 BCE - 476/1453 CE)

From a small city-state to a vast empire spanning three continents, Rome created an extensive legal system, engineering marvels like aqueducts and roads, and a military organization that dominated the ancient world for centuries.

These civilizations didn't exist in isolation. Trade routes connected distant lands, spreading ideas, technologies, and cultural practices. The Silk Road, Mediterranean trade networks, and river systems facilitated exchanges that enriched all participating societies and created the interconnected world that would follow.
